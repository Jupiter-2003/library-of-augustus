GHOST IN THE SHELL: A review
-by Deep Mitra
This review was originally published here: Bitsian Gazette issue 17 (JC BPHC)
IMDb
7.9/10
Rotten Tomatoes
95%
My rating

The year is 2029. Major Motoko Kusanagi, a cyborg federal agent, has been assigned the task of tracking down “The Puppet Master”, a wanted criminal who hacks into and modifies the consciousness of cyborg-human hybrids. This consciousness, encased in a mechanical cyber-brain “Shell”, is called “The Ghost”. Having caused ruckus in Europe and now targeting Japan, the Puppet Master seems to be omnipotent with his capability of controlling any artificial brain. It is now Motoko’s mission to save other cyborgs from their doom and to find out the Puppet Master’s true intentions.

Anybody reading this description would have the same doubt – How is it any different from literally any other anime action movie? Answering this question would be difficult without giving away spoilers, so let’s just say that this movie is not just an action movie but a deeply philosophical and atmospheric piece of art. A film which can boast of reflecting the vision of its director, Mamoru Oshii, in every minute scene.

A feature that really adds to the overall personality of this movie is the love with which the characters have been designed. Not just characters, even weapons. The creators took the decision of not going too hyper-futuristic with the weapon and equipment designs, and went completely old- school. The main intention behind producing such objects is functionality, and they probably didn’t want to compromise with that. This adds to the cyberpunk aesthetic of this film, which is further enhanced by the absolute fluidity in the animation.

Speaking of aesthetics, the soundtrack! Composer Kenji Kawai fused Bulgarian harmonies and Japanese folk music for the theme, which played a huge role in immersing the audience into the world of “Ghost in the Shell”, a world inspired by the musical chaos of Asian cities, where the modern and the ancient co-exist.

The theme “Making of a Cyborg” plays out during the opening credits with the visuals of the making of a cyborg. The haunting Japanese vocals breathe life into that world, a world where humanity seems keen to wipe out even the tiniest hints of their mortality.

However, this upgrade comes at a cost and that is exploited by the main antagonist of this movie. Since the human body has become replaceable, attaching one’s identity to it becomes an issue. This is the age-old paradox of the ship of Theseus. It goes something like this: to preserve the ship of Theseus, the old planks were constantly removed and replaced by fresh wood, but with none of the original parts existing, is the ship still the actual ship of Theseus?

This dilemma leaves only one thing that a cyborg could refer to as their identity; their ghost. The Puppet Master targets these very ghosts, seemingly to use them for his own motives. But with such tampering of the consciousness, is it still the same individual? Or is it just an empty shell? Or was it just a shell from the very beginning, even when it had its own ghost? Such doubts don’t just arise in the minds of the audience. Our protagonist Motoko faces such predicaments herself. One instance of this would be the scene where she keeps staring at the captured shell of a cyborg gone rogue due to the actions of the Puppet Master. She decides to bring her human colleague to the mission whose brain is real (apart from a few cybernetic implants), reflecting her belief that “a system where all the parts react the same way, is a system with a fatal flaw.”

At the time of writing this review, we are closer to the year shown in the movie than the year of its release. Over these near-thirty years, this movie kept influencing several other movies, shows and games due to its visual prowess and existential probes. With such success, it was followed by a great sequel along with shows and a live-action adaptation in 2017 starring Scarlett Johansson. The live-action adaptation was heavily criticized for toning down the heavier themes of the 1995 film into generic victim vs evil organization tropes, but that’s alright. It’s just a different movie, and it’s unfair to blame them for it. The 1995 version also made significant changes to the story and the characterization of Motoko while adapting from the original manga by Masamune Shirow. Thus, the 2017 movie could be treated as a one-time popcorn watch but what instead led to its bombing were bland acting performances and CGI issues.

So, if you are in the mood for a sci-fi anime movie which is less than 90 minutes long, has a substantial amount of action (and violence), has underlying themes of existentialism and is a treat for your eyes - just watch this movie. It might require rewatching as it blatantly avoids spoon-feeding and focuses a lot on the nuances of the human mind. But with each rewatch, you'll unravel another beautiful detail about its story. A good movie always demands the viewers to scratch their heads about it, long after the movie has ended.
